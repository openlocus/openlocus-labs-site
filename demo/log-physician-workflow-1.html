Aging contributes to undernutrition in the elderly population through several mechanisms: 1. **Sarcopenia:** Aging leads to sarcopenia, which is the progressive loss of lean body mass. This loss begins after age 40, with men losing about 10 kg and women about 5 kg of muscle mass over time. Sarcopenia can contribute to undernutrition as it decreases nitrogen balance and increases susceptibility to infections. Reduced physical activity, decreased food intake, increased levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines, hormonal changes, and decreased growth factors all contribute to sarcopenia. 2. **Metabolic Changes:** As people age, there is a natural decline in the basal metabolic rate (BMR), primarily due to a decrease in fat-free mass. Aging also affects overall body composition by increasing the mean body fat as a percentage of body weight, which can affect nutritional status. 3. **Decreased Nutrient Absorption:** Elderly individuals may have a reduced ability to absorb nutrients due to changes in the digestive system, contributing to undernutrition. 4. **Chronic Health Conditions:** Older adults often have chronic health conditions that can affect appetite, alter metabolism, or increase nutrient needs, leading to an imbalance between nutrient intake and the body's demands. 5. **Social and Economic Factors:** The elderly might face social and economic barriers to obtaining adequate nutrition, including financial constraints, lack of access to nutritious food, or reduced ability to shop and prepare meals. 6. **Altered Sensory Perception:** Changes in taste and smell can decrease the desire to eat, leading to reduced food intake and possible undernutrition. 7. **Medications:** Elderly people often take multiple medications that could interfere with nutrient absorption or appetite.
